Electronic Fashion Camp

A three-day camp introducing new technologies that combine concepts from the disciplines of engineering, computer science, fashion and design to girls ages 10-12 years.

I wanted to empower girls with the ability to make an informed choice about their future by exposing them to new technologies that interested them. The NSF claims that during fifth and sixth grade is when a student typically decides if she wants to puruse interests in STEM or not. I developed the Electronic Fashion Camp as a way to show women and girls in my local community that engineering and computer science can mean many things and cross over many fields.

The core building blocks of developing this event were:

  1. Research & Development: I needed to understand a few things before getting started: the LilyPad Arduino, how to engage girls in the development of LilyPad Arduino projects, planning volunteer and participant engagement.
  2. Event & Team Organization: I found participants, volunteers, sponsorship and local support through the network I built during my time in Dallas and through social media. I found women from many fields interested in this work including: engineers, technologists, programmers, business marketers, artists and academics. Over the course of six months leading into the camp, I built workshops to teach them the LilyPad Arduino and establish a sense of community among the volunteers. 
  3. Event Execution: We collaborated as a community of women, utilizing our diverse skillsets throughout the camp. Over the three days, the girls participating completed bonding and sisterhood activities, and heard from women engineers and media experts while designing, prototyping, testing and producing their own LilyPad Arduino project.

Going Forward: For those interested in starting up their own workshops, I want to develop a packaged product and online guides to explain the process. I am also interested in packaging this for educational institutions to use.

LilyPad Arduino Workshop, Design Your World Conference

A two hour workshop introducing girls to: Women Engineers, the Software Development Lifecycle, Wearables and the LilyPad Arduino, a microprocessor board designed for e-textiles. Girls work in groups of 2-3 to complete basic exercises in Sketch, the Arduino programming environment.

While earning my Masters in Emerging Media and Communications (EMAC), I volunteered to help plan the first annual Design Your World Conference, a STEM conference for girls. I was interested in all things new media and decided to use my skills to run the Marketing and Participant teams.

After we built out the structure of the conference, I found another opportunity to help out in planning one of the many workshops offered during the conference. I used the research I was currently completing in my last year of the EMAC program as inspiration and produced a LilyPad Arduino workshop.

The workshop consisted of a presentation by a fellow woman engineer, an explination of what the LilyPad Arduino is, what it can do and how it works, followed by a tinkering session where the girls made LEDs blink; we provided other sensors and actuators for the girls to choose from and explore further.

2010 SWE Region C Conference

The Society of Women Engineers (SWE) hosts one national conference and many annual regional conferences each year. University SWE sections submit bids to host the conference in their region and in collaboration with the local professional sections, plan the next years conference if they win.

For the 2010 SWE Region C Conference, we secured a budget of $60,000 and produced a three day conference for over 300 attendees.

I served as Chair of the 2010 Society of Women Engineers Region C Conference over a two year period. I managed and supported several volunteer committees throughout the planning and execution phases of the conference.

Support provided includes:

  • Worked with over 30 local and national companies to raise a $60,000 corporate sponsorship budget
  • Negotiated vendor and hotel arrangements
  • Booked tours and attractions
  • Coordinated workshop sessions, meals, and keynote dinner at the Dallas World Aquarium
  • Managed participant and speaker registration process
  • Ran conference marketing campaigns leveraging social media
  • Designed all print materials (all program materials, conference swag, logo, t-shirts, banners and signs)
  • Developed the conference website and maintained our social media presence
  • Lead writer of the initial bid to host the conference

Other positions held in SWE:

  • Secretary (NY Professional section), 2013-2014
  • President (UT Dallas Collegiate section), 2008-2009
  • Membership Chair (UT Dallas Collegiate section, 2007-2008

EMAC Mobile Lab

The purpose of this research was to produce creative and useful graphic displays of data received from various potential sensors on public service vehicles.

Our scope included distinct variations of public transportation and emergency response vehicles. We researched sensors, vehicle usage, and data visualizations then developed a list of possible sensors alongside compelling and effective graphic solutions.

First Person Cultural Trainer

First Person Cultural Trainer (FPCT) is an interactive training simulation sponsored by TRADOC G2 Intelligence Support Activity. The user is inserted into different areas in Iraq and Afghanistan and learns to establish themselves within the community. The user builds trust with locals based on conversations and actions with the ultimate goal of learning about IEDs and other threats.
